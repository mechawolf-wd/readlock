# LESSON STYLE - QUICK REFERENCE CHEAT SHEET

## THE CORE FORMULA

### Opening Structure (First 200-250 words)

1. **Hook** → Relatable question or scenario
2. **Problem** → Why current approach fails
3. **Solution Preview** → "But that's where X comes in"
4. **Book Introduction** → "In these lessons, we're going to discover..."
5. **Teaser Bullets** → 3 intriguing takeaways

### Middle Structure (8-12 "Lessons")

Each lesson = **Story → Principle → Application**

- Open with hook or question
- Tell a story (2-5 sentences)
- Extract the lesson (1-2 sentences)
- Apply to reader (1-2 sentences)
- Transition smoothly

### Closing Structure (150-250 words)

1. **Summary** → "The key message in these lessons..."
2. **Principles** → Numbered list (optional)
3. **Actionable Advice** → One practical takeaway
4. **Further Reading** → Related book (optional)

---

## THE TONE IN 10 WORDS

Conversational. Warm. Smart. Encouraging. Clear. Direct. Story-driven. Practical. Accessible. Confident.

---

## MOST COMMON OPENING PATTERNS

**Pattern 1: The Question**
"Have you ever wondered [X]? / What do you really want [Y]?"

**Pattern 2: The Scenario**  
"Imagine you're [situation]... / We live in an era where..."

**Pattern 3: The Desire**
"A great number of us wish for [X]... / We all want [Y]..."

**Always follow with:**
"But [problem]. That's where [solution] comes in."

## SIGNATURE TRANSITION PHRASES

**Starting Sections:**

- "Let's begin with..."
- "Consider this..."
- "Here's a story..."
- "Imagine that..."

**Connecting Ideas:**

- "So what can you do?"
- "This is where [X] comes in"
- "As we've seen..."
- "OK, now that you understand..."

**Explaining:**

- "In other words..."
- "Put simply..."
- "This means that..."
- "The way to [X] is to [Y]"

**Concluding:**

- "This illustrates..."
- "This shows us that..."
- "The key insight here is..."
- "And that's why..."

## THE STORYTELLING FORMULA

### Real Person Stories

"Consider [Name]. [Brief context]. [What they did]. [What happened]. [Lesson extracted]."

**Example:**
"Consider Dave Mustaine. In 1983, he was thrown out of his band. He formed Megadeth and sold 25 million records. Yet he remained unhappy because he compared himself to Metallica. This shows that measuring success against others leads to disappointment."

### Scientific Studies

"In [year], [scientist] tried to answer this question. [Experiment setup]. [Results]. [Discovery]."

**Example:**
"In the 19th century, Edward Thorndike placed cats in a black box. After 20-30 attempts, cats escaped in just six seconds. Thorndike discovered that satisfying behaviors become automatic."

### Hypothetical Scenarios

"Let's say you want to [goal]. [Current situation]. But now that you know [principle], you can [solution]."

**Example:**
"Let's say you want to learn guitar. You struggle to practice daily. But now that you know about implementation intentions, you can commit to practicing Monday, Wednesday, and Friday at 7 AM."

---

## CREDIBILITY WITHOUT BEING ACADEMIC

**Do This:**
✓ "Studies have shown that..."
✓ "Research indicates..."
✓ "Scientists have found..."
✓ "Analysis of 25,000 life stories revealed..."

**Not This:**
✗ "According to Smith et al. (2019)..."
✗ "The literature demonstrates..."
✗ "Empirical evidence suggests (p<0.05)..."

---

## THE ENGAGEMENT TOOLKIT

### Make Readers Feel Smart

- "You've probably worked out..."
- "As you may have guessed..."
- "You might be wondering..."

### Create Partnership

- "Together, we're going to discover..."
- "We all have..."
- "Let's explore..."

### Use Rhetorical Questions

- "So why did this happen?" (Then answer)
- "What can you do?" (Then provide solution)
- "Makes sense, right?" (After explanation)

### Provide Contrast

- "Most people think [X]. But actually [Y]."
- "Sure, [concession], but [main point]."
- "On one hand [X]. On the other hand [Y]."

## PARAGRAPH LENGTH GUIDE

**1-2 sentences** → Transition or emphasis
**3-5 sentences** → Standard explanation (most common)
**6-8 sentences** → Complex concept or story
**1 sentence alone** → Major insight or turning point

**Rhythm Pattern:**
Short → Long → Medium → Short → Long
(Varies naturally, never monotonous)

---

## WORD CHOICE PREFERENCES

**Favor:**

- Discover, achieve, build, create
- Transform, realize, pursue
- Struggle, flourish, thrive
- Powerful, satisfying, remarkable

**Avoid:**

- Utilize → Use
- Commence → Start
- Endeavor → Try
- Facilitate → Help
- Academic jargon

**Emotional Words (Strategic Use):**

- Problems: desperate, frustrated, confused
- Solutions: satisfying, delightful, powerful
- Challenges: harsh, difficult, devastating
- Achievements: remarkable, impressive, extraordinary

---

## THE CONTRAST TECHNIQUE

**Formula:**
"Most people believe [common wisdom]. But it's actually [surprising truth] that [real effect]."

**Examples:**

- "Competition seems ideal. But monopolies actually drive innovation."
- "We think we need big changes. But tiny habits lead to remarkable results."
- "Pleasure sounds great. But it's actually the priority of addicts."

**Use for:**

- Counter-intuitive insights
- Challenging assumptions
- Creating "aha" moments

---

## NUMBERING & LISTS

**When to Number:**
✓ Sequential steps in a process
✓ Multiple criteria or elements
✓ Final summary of principles
✓ Framework being presented (7 Habits, 4 Elements)

**How to Present:**

- "First/One..." "Second/Two..." "Finally..."
- OR "There are X elements: First... Then... Finally..."
- Always explain each item, never just list

**Don't number:**

- Random examples
- Non-sequential ideas
- Flowing narrative sections

## ENDINGS THAT WORK

### The Summary Pattern

```
"The key message in these lessons:

[2-3 sentence essence]

[Optional: Numbered list of principles]

When you [implement], you'll [benefit]."
```

### The Actionable Advice Pattern

```
"Actionable advice:

[Catchy principle headline]

[2-4 sentences: how to implement]

[Optional: example or final encouragement]"
```

### The Recommendation Pattern

```
"Suggested further reading: [Title] by [Author]

[How it relates or extends the message]"
```

## RED FLAGS TO AVOID

❌ Starting with book title or formal intro
❌ Using passive voice excessively
❌ Academic citations in text
❌ Vague examples without specifics
❌ Missing transitions between sections
❌ Stories without extracted lessons
❌ No practical application
❌ Overly complex sentences
❌ Condescending tone
❌ Ending without actionable advice

---

## THE 3-SENTENCE TEST

**Can you summarize each section in 3 sentences?**

1. **What's the concept?** → Clear principle stated
2. **What's the story/example?** → Concrete illustration
3. **What should I do?** → Application to life

If you can't, the section needs work.

**Body**

- [ ] "You" used frequently?

**Tone**

- [ ] Conversational, not academic?
- [ ] Confident, not arrogant?
- [ ] Reads aloud smoothly?
- [ ] Uses contractions?

**Ending**

- [ ] Clear summary provided?
- [ ] Actionable advice given?
- [ ] Practical, not vague?
- [ ] Leaves reader satisfied?

---

## QUICK QUALITY TEST

**Read your opening paragraph aloud.**

Ask:

1. Would I keep reading?
2. Do I immediately understand the relevance?
3. Does it sound like a friend talking?

If 3 "yes" → Good start
If any "no" → Revise before continuing

---

## MEMORIZE THESE RATIOS

- **60% explanation** : 40% story
- **70% simple words** : 30% sophisticated
- **80% active voice** : 20% passive
- **2:1 ratio** of "you" to "I/we"
- **Every 200 words** = 1 story/example
- **3-5 sentences** = average paragraph

---

## THE ULTIMATE REMINDER

**You're not writing a book report.**
**You're helping a friend understand something valuable.**

Write with:

- ✓ Warmth (like talking to a friend)
- ✓ Clarity (a 15-year-old could understand)
- ✓ Respect (reader is intelligent)
- ✓ Purpose (every word earns its place)
- ✓ Action (reader can apply it today)

---

**Lesson platform's secret:**
Complex → Simple
Abstract → Concrete  
Distant → Immediate
Academic → Conversational
Passive → Active

**Your mantra:**
"Make it clear. Make it concrete. Make it compelling."
