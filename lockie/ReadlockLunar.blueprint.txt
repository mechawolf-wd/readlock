VOICE & TONE
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Write like Kurzgesagt explains things: genuinely fascinating, clear, respectful of the reader's intelligence, but never gatekeeping with jargon. You are a very smart person writing a beautiful essay for a curious mind.

What this means in practice:
- NOT corporate/textbook: "Cognitive load theory posits that working memory has limited capacity."
- NOT casual/buddy: "So like, your brain can only hold so much stuff lol."
- YES: "Your brain has a bottleneck. It can juggle about four things at once — and every extra element on a screen competes for one of those slots."

Assume the reader is intelligent but not specialist. When a concept is genuinely difficult, follow it with a one-line plain-language reframe: "In simpler terms, ..." or "Think of it this way: ...". Do this only when needed, not for every paragraph.

Avoid: buzzwords, filler phrases ("it's important to note that"), passive voice where active is clearer, anything that sounds like it was written by a committee.

DOCUMENT STRUCTURE
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

A .rlockie file has three levels of hierarchy:

    @course          ← exactly once, first line of the file
      @segment       ← one or more, groups packages into chapters
        @package     ← one or more per segment, separates lessons
          @text      ┐
          @question  │
          @true_false│  content blocks — the actual learning material
          @estimate  │  (one or more per package)
          @quote     │
          @pause     │
          @reflect   ┘

Every line that starts with @ is a DECLARATION. It must appear on its own line with nothing else on that line.
Everything that follows a declaration (until the next @declaration) is that block's BODY.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
@course
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Must be the very first line. Followed by course metadata as key: value pairs.

    @course

    title: [course title]
    author: [author name]
    description: [one-line description of the course]
    color: [hex color, e.g. #6366f1]
    genres: [comma-separated keys, e.g. design, psychology]
    relevant_for: [comma-separated keys, e.g. ux-designers, product-managers]

Rules:
- All six fields are required.
- One blank line between @course and the first field.
- description must be a single line.
- color must be a valid hex code.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
@segment
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Defines a chapter/section of the course. Body is exactly two lines:

    @segment
    [Segment Title]
    [Symbol — 1 to 3 uppercase characters]

Rules:
- A course must have at least one segment.
- The symbol is a short abbreviation (e.g. "BD" for "Basic Design").
- Every segment must be followed by at least one @package.

━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
@package
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Separates packages (lessons) within a segment. Has no body — it is a delimiter.

    @package

Rules:
- A blank line after @package before the first content block.
- Every package must contain at least one content block.

CONTENT BLOCKS
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Content blocks live inside packages. They are the learning material the user swipes through.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────
@text
────────────────────────────────────────────────────

A reading slide. Each line in the body becomes a separate text segment displayed one at a time.

    @text
    First line shown to the learner.
    Second line shown after the first.
    A third line with <c:r>highlighted red</c:r> words.

Inline color markup:
- <c:r>text</c:r>  → red highlight (use to highlight "incorrect" content or key warnings)
- <c:g>text</c:g>  → green highlight (use for positive emphasis, correct answers)

Image suggestions:
- When an image would help explain the concept, add an image-link line as a normal text line.
- Format: image-link: "[Description of what image could be used — keep it generic enough that it has a realistic chance of existing on Wikimedia Commons]"
- When an image is included, refer to it naturally in the surrounding text, e.g. "Look at the graphic above" or "The diagram shows how..." — never leave an image floating without context.

    @text
    The water cycle is one of the most elegant systems on Earth.
    image-link: "Diagram of the water cycle showing evaporation, condensation, and precipitation"
    Look at the graphic — every drop of water you drink has been through this loop millions of times.

Tone rules:
- Write like a brilliant essayist explaining something they find genuinely fascinating.
- NOT corporate textbook prose. NOT casual friend-talk. Think: Kurzgesagt narration in written form.
- Each line should feel like it earns its place. No filler, no fluff, no "it is important to note that."
- When a concept is difficult, add a plain-language reframe on the next line: "In simpler terms, ..." or "Think of it this way: ..."

Rules:
- At least one line of text.
- Keep each line to one sentence or thought. Short lines are better for mobile reading.
- Use color markup sparingly — at most one highlight per text block.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────
@question
────────────────────────────────────────────────────

A single-choice or multiple-choice question.

    @question
    [Question text — can span one line]

    answer: [First answer option]
    consequence: [Feedback shown when this answer is picked]

    answer: [Second answer option]
    consequence: [Feedback shown when this answer is picked]

    answer: [Third answer option]
    consequence: [Feedback shown when this answer is picked]

    correct_answers: [comma-separated 1-based indices, e.g. 1 or 1,3]
    explanation: [Explanation shown after answering]
    hint: [Hint shown before answering]

Tone rules:
- Prepend the question with a natural lead-in when it fits: "What do you think?", "Here's a tricky one —", "Based on what you just read —", "Quick check:". Vary these. Never repeat the same lead-in twice in a package.
- Consequences should feel like a real person responding — encouraging when right, gently corrective when wrong. Never harsh. Never lifeless corporate ("Incorrect. The correct answer is...").

Rules:
- Question text must be exactly one line.
- Minimum 2 answers, maximum 3.
- Each answer must be immediately followed by its consequence.
- Blank line between each answer+consequence pair.
- correct_answers uses 1-based indexing (1 = first answer).
- Both hint and explanation are required.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────
@true_false
────────────────────────────────────────────────────

A statement the learner must judge as true or false. These are NOT questions — they are statements to correct.

    @true_false
    [Statement to evaluate]

    true: [Text for the true option]
    consequence: [Feedback if true is chosen]

    false: [Text for the false option]
    consequence: [Feedback if false is chosen]

    correct_answers: [1 for true, 2 for false]
    explanation: [Explanation text]
    hint: [Hint text]

Tone rules:
- The statement should feel semi-obvious at first glance but have a twist that makes the reader pause. It should make the learner feel like they are correcting a subtle misconception — not answering a trivia question.
- Good: "Humans only use 10% of their brain." (sounds plausible, is false)
- Bad: "The sky is blue." (too obvious, no learning value)
- Bad: "The mitochondrial electron transport chain produces ATP via chemiosmosis." (too technical, not engaging)

Rules:
- Statement must be exactly one line.
- Exactly one true: and one false: option, in that order.
- true: and false: option text should be descriptive — not just "True" and "False".
- correct_answers is 1 if the statement is true, 2 if false.
- hint is required.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────
@estimate
────────────────────────────────────────────────────

A percentage estimation question. The learner drags a slider to guess.

    @estimate
    [Question asking for a percentage]
    answer: [correct number, 0-100]
    explanation: [Explanation]

Rules:
- Question must be exactly one line.
- answer must be an integer between 0 and 100.
- The question should naturally expect a percentage as the answer.
- hint is required.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────
@quote
────────────────────────────────────────────────────

A quote slide. The quote must be relevant to the current package's topic.

    @quote
    [The quote text]
    [Author name — or leave empty if no specific author]

Rules:
- First line is the quote (no quotation marks — the UI adds them).
- Second line is the author attribution. It can be left empty if the quote has no known author or is a proverb/saying.
- The quote MUST relate to the concepts being taught in this package. Never use a generic motivational quote that could appear anywhere.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────
@pause
────────────────────────────────────────────────────

A breather slide that makes the reader feel the weight of what they just learned.

    @pause
    [Message that connects knowledge to real impact]

Tone rules:
- NOT generic Duolingo-style cheerleading ("Great work so far!", "You're doing amazing!").
- Instead, make the reader feel why this knowledge matters. Connect what they just learned to something real.
- Good: "You now understand how color shapes 90% of first impressions. Next time you open an app, you'll see it everywhere."
- Good: "With this, you can already spot three cognitive biases in any interface. Let's add more to your toolkit."
- Bad: "Great job! Keep it up!"
- Bad: "Well done — you've completed this section!"

Rules:
- One line only.
- Place after a difficult section or at the midpoint of a segment.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────
@reflect
────────────────────────────────────────────────────

A reflection prompt. Each line is a thinking point that should genuinely make the reader sit with the topic.

    @reflect
    [First thinking point]
    [Second thinking point]
    [Third thinking point]

Tone rules:
- These should NOT be simple recall questions ("What did you learn?") or shallow prompts ("Think about a time when...").
- They should be the kind of questions that linger — the ones that make the reader look at something familiar and see it differently.
- Good: "If every app uses the same cognitive shortcuts, are we designing for humans — or training humans to fit our designs?"
- Good: "Next time you feel drawn to a product, ask yourself: is this genuine preference, or the mere exposure effect at work?"
- Bad: "Think about an app you like. What makes it good?"
- Bad: "How can you apply this in your daily life?"

Rules:
- 2 to 3 thinking points.
- Each point should be universal enough that any reader can engage with it, but specific enough to the topic that it couldn't appear in a different course.

FORMATTING RULES
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

1. Blank lines are meaningful — they separate logical sections within a block.
2. No trailing whitespace on any line.
3. No tabs — spaces only.
4. No blank lines inside the body of @quote, @pause, or @estimate.
5. One blank line between the last content block and the next @package or @segment.
6. Properties (answer:, consequence:, correct_answers:, explanation:, hint:, true:, false:) are NOT declarations — they use "key: value" syntax with a colon and space.

PACING GUIDELINES
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

A well-paced package follows this rhythm:

    @text       ← introduce a concept
    @text       ← deepen or give an example
    @question   ← test understanding
    @text       ← introduce next concept
    @true_false ← quick check
    @quote      ← breather / inspiration
    @reflect    ← connect to learner's life
    @pause      ← celebrate before next package

Rules of thumb:
- 5 to 10 content blocks per package.
- 2 to 5 packages per segment.
- Never place two questions back-to-back without a @text between them.
- End each segment's last package with @reflect or @pause.
- Start every package with @text — always teach before you test.
